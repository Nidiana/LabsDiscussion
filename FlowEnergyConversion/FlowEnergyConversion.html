In the throat (P4–P6) static pressure is at its lowest and dynamic pressure is at its highest. If you doubled the fan speed, how would the shape of the three curves change — and how would their magnitudes change?

The shape of the curves would stay the same — the throat would still be the fastest and lowest-pressure point, and the diffuser would still show pressure recovery. This is because the shape depends only on the geometry of the duct, not on how fast the fan runs. The magnitudes would increase significantly. Dynamic pressure depends on velocity squared (q = ½ρV²), so doubling the speed quadruples the dynamic pressure at every point, and the static pressure differences also quadruple. In engineering this matters: a wind tunnel running at twice the speed needs a fan with four times the pressure capability, and the structural loads on the duct walls are four times larger.
