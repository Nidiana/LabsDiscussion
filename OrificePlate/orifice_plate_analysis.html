Experimental Data Collection

For each trial, set a different flow rate using the bench valve, wait for steady conditions, then record \(\Delta P\) in mmH‚ÇÇO from the digital manometer and \(Q\) in L/min from the rotameter. Aim for 4‚Äì6 evenly spread flow rates across the available range.

What are you measuring ‚Äî and why?

The manometer measures one thing only: the pressure difference \(\Delta P = P_1 - P_2\) between the upstream pipe and the orifice throat. There are no individual pressure taps, so absolute static pressures are not available.

Bernoulli's equation for an ideal (inviscid, incompressible, steady) fluid says that pressure drop is entirely converted into kinetic energy gain:

\[\Delta P = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho\!\left(V_2^2 - V_1^2\right)\]

Because flow rate is constant at every cross-section (\(Q = A_1 V_1 = A_2 V_2\)), combining Bernoulli with continuity gives the ideal predicted flow rate:

\[Q_\text{ideal} = A_2\,\sqrt{\dfrac{2\,\Delta P}{\rho\,(1-\beta^4)}} \qquad \beta = \frac{D_2}{D_1} = 0.60\]

Your rotameter gives \(Q_\text{actual}\). Recording both across several flow rates lets you determine the coefficient of discharge \(C_d = Q_\text{actual}/Q_\text{ideal}\) ‚Äî a measure of how much energy is lost at the sharp orifice edge and in the turbulent flow downstream of it. For a sharp-edged orifice plate, you can expect \(C_d \approx 0.60\text{‚Äì}0.67\). The quiz will ask you to explain why \(C_d < 1\) and why this matters.

Bernoulli Energy Budget ‚Äî Selected Trial

For an ideal fluid, Bernoulli requires the measured pressure drop to be exactly consumed by the gain in kinetic energy: \(\Delta P = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho(V_2^2 - V_1^2)\). This chart compares the two for a selected trial. The shortfall ‚Äî \(\Delta P\) exceeding the kinetic energy gain ‚Äî is the energy lost to turbulent mixing just downstream of the orifice edge. The permanent (irrecoverable) pressure loss is estimated as \(h_L = \Delta P\,(1 - C_d^2)\).

Permanent Pressure Loss \(h_L\) vs Flow Rate ‚Äî All Trials

\(h_L = \Delta P\,(1 - C_d^2)\) is the energy permanently lost to turbulence at the orifice ‚Äî the portion of \(\Delta P\) that is not recovered downstream. Unlike a venturi, there is no gradual diffuser to reclaim it. Each bar is one trial. As \(Q\) rises, \(h_L\) grows rapidly ‚Äî making the running cost of an orifice plate significant at high flow rates.

Question 4 ‚Äî Orifice plate vs venturi: why is Cd lower?

How does \(C_d\) for the orifice plate compare with a venturi meter, and why?

Typical values:  Venturi \(C_d \approx 0.95\text{‚Äì}0.99\)  |  Sharp-edged orifice plate \(C_d \approx 0.61\text{‚Äì}0.67\)

The diagram below shows what happens at the orifice edge. Because water cannot turn a sharp right-angle corner, the jet continues to contract past the orifice, reaching its narrowest point (the contracted jet, historically called the vena contracta ‚Äî Latin for "contracted vein") a short distance downstream. The actual flow area at this minimum is smaller than the orifice area \(A_2\), which is why \(Q_\text{ideal}\) ‚Äî which assumes full use of \(A_2\) ‚Äî overpredicts the flow.

The jet narrows past the hole to a minimum cross-section (the contracted jet), then re-expands turbulently ‚Äî permanently wasting energy as heat.
