Step 01

What Is a Vacuum Tube Solar Collector?

Just-In-Time Theory

A vacuum solar collector (or evacuated-tube collector) is a high-efficiency device that captures solar radiation and converts it to useful heat. Each tube contains an inner absorber coated with a selective surface, surrounded by an outer glass tube from which air has been removed to create a vacuum.

The vacuum is key: it eliminates convective heat loss between the absorber and the environment, dramatically improving performance — especially in cold or windy climates. Most residential vacuum collectors achieve optical efficiencies of 70–80 % and can reach stagnation temperatures above 200 °C.

Step 02

The Collector Efficiency Equation

Just-In-Time Theory

Collector efficiency η describes how much of the incident solar energy is actually delivered as useful heat. The Hottel–Whillier–Bliss (HWB) equation, used in EN ISO 9806, expresses this as a function of two loss coefficients:

Hottel–Whillier–Bliss Model
η = η₀ − a₁ · (ΔT / G) − a₂ · (ΔT² / G)
η₀ = zero-loss efficiency (optical efficiency)  |  a₁ = first-order heat-loss coefficient (W/m²K)  |  a₂ = second-order heat-loss coefficient (W/m²K²)  |  ΔT = Tm − Tamb (K)  |  G = solar irradiance (W/m²)

ΔT/G is the reduced temperature difference — the fundamental x-axis of any collector performance curve. At ΔT = 0 (fluid inlet = ambient), η equals η₀. As the collector runs hotter relative to ambient, losses grow and η falls.

Typical vacuum collector values: η₀ ≈ 0.72, a₁ ≈ 1.0 W/m²K, a₂ ≈ 0.005 W/m²K². Compare this with a flat-plate: a₁ ≈ 3–4 W/m²K — much higher losses!

Step 04

Live Performance Curve

Just-In-Time Theory

The collector performance curve plots η vs ΔT/G. Its shape tells you everything about the collector's thermal behaviour. A steep negative slope means high losses (poor insulation). Vacuum collectors have a near-flat curve even at elevated temperatures — that is their defining advantage over flat-plates.

The stagnation temperature (where η = 0) is the maximum temperature the collector can reach when no heat is extracted. For vacuum collectors this can exceed 200 °C, which creates important safety design considerations.

Step 05

System Sizing & Annual Useful Heat

Just-In-Time Theory

The annual useful heat delivered by a collector field depends on three factors: collector area A, mean annual irradiation H, and the system efficiency ηsys (accounting for real operating temperatures, piping losses, and storage losses).

Annual Useful Heat Yield
Qannual = ηsys · H · A
Qannual in kWh/yr  |  ηsys dimensionless (typically 0.35–0.55 for DHW systems)  |  H in kWh/(m²·yr) — annual global horizontal irradiation  |  A in m² — gross collector area

A typical UK location receives H ≈ 1 000 kWh/(m²·yr). A 4 m² vacuum collector with ηsys = 0.45 delivers about 1 800 kWh/yr — covering ~60 % of a household's hot water demand. Southern European locations (H ≈ 1 700 kWh/m²·yr) would deliver ~3 000 kWh/yr.
